Overall UI Concept & Layout

Visual / UX

This is the foundational decision for Crate Listen — the layout pattern, navigation approach, and visual density that will define the entire user experience. Each concept uses the same dark minimal palette (#0a0a0f background, #06b6d4 cyan accent) but takes a very different approach to how users navigate, discover music, and interact with the player. Think about where your users will primarily use this: desktop at home? Phone on the go? Both equally?

The classic Spotify layout adapted to our dark theme. A narrow icon sidebar (56px) on the left for primary navigation, with the main content area taking up most of the screen. A persistent bottom bar shows now-playing with mini controls. Clicking the bottom bar opens a fullscreen player. This is the layout most music app users already know — zero learning curve. Desktop-optimized but works on tablets.

Works well when

  • Users primarily use desktop/laptop
  • Maximum familiarity desired
  • Lots of content to navigate

Watch out for

  • Poor mobile experience without major adaptation
  • Sidebar wastes space on narrow screens

This is my recommendation. A mobile-first design with bottom tab navigation (Home, Explore, Library, Shows) that scales beautifully to desktop via a responsive layout. The "now playing" card is integrated into the home feed rather than a separate bar — tapping it opens the fullscreen player. Filter pills at the top let users quickly switch context. This approach ensures the app feels native on phones (where most music listening happens) while still working great on desktop with wider content areas.

Works well when

  • Mobile is the primary use case
  • PWA installable experience matters
  • Clean, modern feel desired

Watch out for

  • Desktop may feel "spacious" without sidebar density
  • Bottom nav less common on web (mobile pattern)

Album art and the current track dominate the screen. The background bleeds with a gradient extracted from the cover art (like Apple Music). Navigation is a floating glass-morphism pill at the bottom, and the now-playing section is always visible at the top with large artwork. The feed below mixes new releases, shows, and friend activity into a unified timeline. This creates a visually striking, magazine-like experience where the music itself is the UI.

Works well when

  • Visual impact is the #1 priority
  • Users care deeply about album art / aesthetics
  • Small library where discovery is curated

Watch out for

  • Less space for content density
  • Glassmorphism can hurt readability
  • Performance cost of blurs on low-end devices

A power-user layout with a persistent left panel for browsing (artists, queue, playlists) and a detail panel on the right showing the selected content. Think of it like a file manager for music — click an artist on the left, see their albums on the right. The mini player lives in the bottom-left corner. This is the most information-dense option, ideal for users who browse their library extensively and want to see as much data as possible. Tabs in the right panel (Albums/Tracks/Similar) reduce context-switching.

Works well when

  • Power users with large libraries (900+ artists)
  • Desktop-primary usage
  • Maximum information density

Watch out for

  • Not viable on mobile — needs a completely different mobile layout
  • Can feel like an admin tool, not a consumer app
  • Higher complexity to build responsive version
